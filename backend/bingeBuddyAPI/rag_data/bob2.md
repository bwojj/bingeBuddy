# Episode 2 — The Real Cause of Binge Eating: The Urges

*The Brain over Binge Podcast — Katherine Hansen and Cookie Rosenbloom*

---

## Welcome

**Katherine:** Welcome to the Brain over Binge podcast, where we share a simple brain-based approach to ending binge eating. This is Katherine Hansen, author of *Brain over Binge* and *The Brain over Binge Recovery Guide*, and I'm so glad to be co-hosting this show with my friend and colleague Cookie Rosenbloom, who is a master coach and an author herself.

**Cookie:** Hi Katherine, I'm happy to be here, and I'm happy to be with all of you listeners.

**Katherine:** The purpose of this podcast is to share information and insights to help you stop binge eating. With each episode we want to empower you and encourage you and help you move toward freedom from binge eating.

Today we're going to be talking about the real cause of binge eating. Those of you struggling with binge eating have likely looked for a cause. You've probably asked yourself why. Why am I doing this over and over again when I know it's causing pain in my life? The very simple answer is that you binge because you have urges to binge. It's the urges that are the problem.

In the first episode I explained that this is a central concept of the Brain over Binge approach. Today we're going to dive deeper, so that you can understand the urges and eventually overcome them.

## How conventional treatment views the urges

**Katherine:** The first thing I want to do is highlight the difference between the way we view urges in the Brain over Binge approach and the way urges are viewed in more conventional forms of treatment.

In conventional treatment it's common to hear that the urges to binge are symptoms of underlying emotional or psychological problems. This is a very popular and mainstream idea. The theory is that you have urges to binge because you're not able to cope with those problems or with difficult emotions in an effective way. Because binge eating is seen as a coping mechanism, the urges are viewed in a more symbolic way, like a signal that you need to learn to cope better.

This is what I was taught, but to me the urges never felt like a need to cope. They always felt like a need to eat massive quantities of food, at a very basic level. It didn't seem any more complicated than that, and it wasn't.

If you've been using the theory that binge eating is a coping mechanism, you probably believe that to recover you need to learn healthy coping behaviors in order to make your desire to binge go away. For example, if you binge when you're alone, you may think the cause of your binges is loneliness and that you're binge eating to cope with the negative feelings of being lonely, so you may try to find other ways to deal with loneliness, hoping you won't feel the urges anymore. It's the same with depression or anxiety.

If using this theory does take your urges away, that's great. If it's helping you, there's no need to abandon it. But the reality is this is not a very effective strategy, and it likely hasn't been helpful for most of you listening. You're definitely not alone in this.

Even if you manage to become less lonely, less anxious or less depressed, the urges will still appear, because the urges aren't always predictable. They can appear in so many different situations and under countless circumstances, even when you're experiencing positive emotions. Also, trying to solve other issues hoping the urges will go away can become a monumental task that simply doesn't work. Furthermore, many of the problems you're experiencing may actually be the result of the binge eating itself. It can seem nearly impossible to solve an issue like depression, anxiety or loneliness when it's actually the binge eating that's making you depressed, anxious and lonely.

**Cookie:** We know that many of you are lovers of all types of self-help material, as I am too. But one of the things that's important for you to know very early in learning about this approach is this: it may improve your life if you learn how to deal with moods, and it may improve your life if you learn how to solve problems and live a better life. We know that. But the most important thing is to separate improving yourself and improving your life from stopping binge eating. It is not necessary to be the best you can be in order to stop this problem.

You can stop bingeing without perfecting all these different areas of your life, because we see the urges themselves as the problem. When you look at it that way, the answer is to stop responding to the urges, and eventually they will fade away.

## Defining the urge

**Cookie:** The way that Katherine defines the urges to binge in her books is: any thought, feeling or physical sensation that encourages binge eating. These urges can be very compelling and very powerful, or they can be a little more subtle. They're any internal state you experience that encourages you to binge.

We're going to teach you how to very quickly recognize your urges, whether they come in the form of a physical sensation, a thought or a feeling. When you recognize them, not responding to them is going to become easier and easier.

**Katherine:** I completely agree. That definition is so important. Anything that encourages binge eating is something you don't need to respond to.

## Why you have urges at all

**Katherine:** Knowing that the urges are the problem simplifies recovery, but a lot of you may be thinking, well, why do I have these urges to begin with? You may think, okay, it makes sense that the urges are the direct cause of each binge, but the fact that I have these urges must mean I'm flawed or diseased in some way. It doesn't. The development of the urges is a rather natural consequence of being human with the brain that we have, and especially being human with the brain we have in a world that's often obsessed with body image and dieting.

The urges usually develop for two very simple reasons.

### 1. Survival instincts

The first is survival instincts, and this is typically brought about by restrictive dieting. Binge eating is actually an adaptive response to food restriction. If you go on a weight loss diet and limit your calorie intake, you put your body and brain in a survival-oriented state, causing you to crave and seek out large amounts of food, especially highly stimulating or highly palatable food like foods high in sugar, unhealthy fats and carbohydrates.

This survival state is actually a symptom of a healthy brain, not a sign of disease. It's simply your brain trying to protect you from what it perceives as starvation, and trying to store up calories.

This is what happened in my case. I restricted my food intake in an attempt to control my weight, and then I found myself thinking about food more than I ever had in my life. My cravings increased and increased until one day it felt like my primitive brain completely took over and I binged for the first time. I didn't understand what was happening and it felt totally out of control. I thought it was because I was weak or something was wrong with me, but in reality it was simply because I'd put my body and brain in a state where all it cared about was getting as much food as possible to protect me from starvation. There wasn't actually anything wrong with me.

### 2. Habit formation

After my first binge I tried to continue dieting, but the binge eating kept happening over and over, which leads me to the second reason that urges develop: habit formation.

Once you binge many times, your body and brain become conditioned to expect and demand these binges, so your brain automatically sends out strong urges as if binge eating is necessary for your survival. These urges are just a signal that your brain currently thinks you must binge to survive.

The binge eating habit can develop after repeated survival-driven binges, which is the most common scenario and what happened in my case. Or it can develop after a period of overeating that increases over time and develops into binge eating.

Habit formation is basically when the primitive part of your brain becomes conditioned to react as if binges are necessary to keep you alive. Once this habit is in place, the primitive brain will keep producing urges even if you're no longer restricting your food intake, because it senses that you need the habit to live.

But this also is not a sign of disease. Habit formation is a vital brain function and we wouldn't be able to live without it. We need the ability to develop and maintain habits. The problem is that habit formation has a dark side: it fuels destructive habits as well as productive ones.

## What about risk factors?

**Katherine:** There are some potential risk factors that could help explain why some people develop the binge eating habit after a period of dieting or overeating and some do not. It's common to hear that factors like low self-esteem, cultural pressure, past trauma, genetics or personality traits might be to blame. It's not that none of this has any validity, but we want you to know that there's nothing truly conclusive about any of these factors. Some people have these problems and a genetic susceptibility and never develop eating disorders. Some people don't have these issues and do develop binge eating.

Spending a lot of time trying to figure out what made you different from someone who did not develop the habit is not very practical. If you think you have to completely figure out the problem before you can solve it, it can keep you stuck.

With our simple brain-based explanation of why you have urges, we want you to realize that you are not fundamentally flawed, and we want you to feel empowered that you can begin to make change. By making change, you actually have the amazing opportunity to rewire your brain and erase this habit.

## Neuroplasticity

**Cookie:** Now that you understand where the urges came from, it's interesting to think about them, because in order for them to give you this habit of binge eating, you have had to act on those urges over and over again. And the beautiful, simple thing here is that in order to recover, all you have to do is learn how to not act on those urges over and over again.

When you learn to do this, you're actually utilizing something called neuroplasticity to rewire your brain and erase the habit. Neuroplasticity simply means the ability of your brain to change, to change the pathways in it that lead you to do certain things and to have habitual responses. When you do something over and over again you form a pathway. When the urge comes up and you don't respond, that pathway will be pruned away.

So it's not so important to try to recover by figuring out why you in particular have your urges, but to ask yourself: what can I do to eliminate the urges? We see that as the whole source of the problem and the solution.

**Katherine:** That's right. And to eliminate them, you have to stop acting on them. Right now you're probably acting on these urges because they make you feel so uncomfortable. In effect, you're bingeing just to cope with the urges to binge, because they create such a strong desire and make you feel like you have no choice. The only way to get rid of the urge for you right now is to binge, but that's not what you want to do, because it's going to perpetuate the habit and keep you in this cycle.

So what we try to teach people is to view urges in a new way, so that you don't feel so compelled to act, they don't feel so uncomfortable, and you can allow them to pass. As they pass, it utilizes neuroplasticity to rewire your brain, to teach your brain you no longer need this habit to survive, and the brain turns off the urges.

## Common question: aren't urges a symptom of something deeper?

**Katherine:** One of the questions that comes up when people hear that the urges are the problem relates to the fact that many of you have spent time in more traditional approaches and have heard that urges are just a symptom of underlying issues or of a disease.

As we alluded to earlier, this is not usually helpful, because it makes you feel like you have to solve all your other problems to make the urges go away. And even if you do manage to solve the problems, the urges usually don't go away because of the force of habit.

We want you to be open to the idea that these urges aren't symptoms of anything, that they are the problem in and of themselves. They are a symptom that you have created a habit in your brain, or a symptom that you are in a survival state where you've restricted calories and need large amounts of food. You do not need to binge when you're in that survival state, but you do need to eat adequately and enough.

In the last episode we talked about the two recovery goals of this approach: one is to dismiss the urges to binge, and two is to eat adequately. If you are eating adequately, it allows the urges to fade away more easily when you stop acting on them.

**Cookie:** We know that most older literature talks about the urges as symptoms of all different types of emotional issues, but we believe that viewing your urges as a symptom of something deeper and bigger underlying your entire life could make your recovery confusing and way more complicated than it needs to be.

Sometimes people believe that their binge eating developed as a result of an emotional problem, like low self-esteem, not feeling good about yourself, family problems or mood issues. But even if you had those problems at the same time you started to binge, and somehow they became connected, and you conditioned yourself so that when you felt this, you also started to binge, and the two got linked together, even if you believe there was an original connection, the urges to binge themselves are still the reason for each time you binge.

So that's all we really need to work on together, because without those urges to binge, you would not binge. Am I right? Regardless of whatever else was going on in your life, if you did not have urges, you would not binge.

Once the habit develops, the emotional issues that were present when you first started feel like they're linked together, but what's really driving the binges are those urges. If you didn't have the urges and you were upset about a situation in your life, without the urges you would never say, huh, I think I'll binge today, that might solve this problem. We would never want that, because we know what it does to how we feel about ourselves and the rest of our life. So it's the urges that we're really going to focus on.

**Katherine and Cookie:** You have the power to rewire your brain and live a binge-free life.
