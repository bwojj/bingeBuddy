## What binge eating actually is

**Katherine:** Before we get into teaching you how to stop this behavior, we want to talk about what binge eating actually is. This is going to help you determine if this podcast is right for you or someone you care about.

Binge eating is a little different for everyone, but it's not the simple overeating that most people do from time to time, like at a holiday or a big meal. It's not just having a little too much dessert or a little too much junk food. Binge eating is a much more compulsive and much more destructive behavior, and it creates so much suffering in your life.

Here is the more clinical definition. A binge eating episode is eating, in a discrete period of time such as a two-hour period, an amount of food that is definitely larger than most people would eat during a similar period of time under similar circumstances. Binge eating episodes are accompanied by a sense of lack of control over what or how much is being eaten.

That sense of lack of control is really key. If you binge, you know it can feel like you're someone else. It can feel like you have no choice but to binge, like you can't stop yourself no matter how hard you try. It affects your life in a negative way. It can lead to shame, regret and frustration. It can affect your work, your relationships, and your ability to live your life the way you want to. It can make you feel depressed, anxious and very isolated.

Some people purge after binge eating, and in that case it's called bulimia. Purging can be self-induced vomiting, laxatives, over-exercise, or restrictive dieting. If purging is not involved, then it's called binge eating disorder, or BED. To be diagnosed with either, the binge eating or the bingeing and purging needs to occur at least one time per week for three months. But for the purposes of our podcast, the frequency doesn't matter. If you're doing it at all, it's causing pain in your life, and the information we're going to share can help you even if you don't have a clinical diagnosis.

## Katherine's story

**Katherine:** I was a binge eater for six years, and I was technically bulimic because I purged through over-exercising and dieting. But binge eating was my primary problem. It was what I felt like I just couldn't get a handle on for so long.

I dieted for two years prior to developing the binge eating, and I also had a brief period of anorexia. When the binge eating started it increased pretty quickly, and it made me feel out of control. I didn't feel like I could stop it on my own, so I ended up seeking help in the form of therapy, which ended up not being helpful for me. A lot of my book is devoted to explaining why the concepts I learned in therapy weren't helpful, and in some ways made things worse. I'm not criticizing therapy across the board, because I realize it has a useful purpose for a lot of people.

In therapy I learned that binge eating was basically a coping mechanism for problems in my life and for difficult emotions. I learned it was my way of dealing with pain or stress, and that it was a symptom of underlying problems like depression, anxiety, low self-esteem, family problems, or things in my past. To recover, I thought I needed to get to the bottom of all of this. I thought I needed to solve all those problems or learn to cope better with all my emotions in order to stop feeling like I wanted to binge. I went down that path for a long time, thinking I needed some sort of fundamental transformation in my personality and in the way I approached life. But despite doing all the right things in therapy, my binge eating only got worse over the years.

Then in 2005 I came across some very useful information about the brain. Learning about the brain simplified recovery for me. It showed me that there wasn't anything fundamentally wrong with me. I wasn't flawed or broken. I had just become a temporary victim of my own brain, my own healthy brain. I also learned that I didn't need to transform in order to recover. I didn't need to solve all my problems first, and I didn't need to learn to cope perfectly with life in order to walk away from the binge eating.

I shared my personal story and how I applied these brain-based concepts in *Brain over Binge* in 2011. After writing that book I began talking to many others who read it and were helped by it, and I began helping hundreds of men and women. I got to see what helped them and where more information was needed. So in 2016 I published *The Brain over Binge Recovery Guide*, a self-help version that helps people apply these concepts in their own life, in their own unique way, so they can create a path to recovery that feels authentic to them.

## Cookie's story

**Cookie:** I have been a weight loss coach for over 25 years, and mostly I focused on emotional eating and general weight loss. I did not have an issue growing up with my own weight. My struggle started as an adult when I had my two daughters and had trouble losing my pregnancy weight.

That frustration led me to become an emotional eater, and it increased and increased until it became the thing I thought about literally day and night. I woke up thinking about it and I went to bed thinking about it: my weight, what I ate, what I didn't eat, whether I was good or bad. I started realizing that by focusing so much on my eating and having such a diet mentality all the time, I began slipping into the world of binge eating. I began getting urges to eat large quantities of food just randomly, because my whole world was so focused on restricting and watching what I ate to lose that baby weight.

I was really miserable, and I was a weight loss coach at the time, so nobody knew what I was going through. I had a lot of shame. I realized one day that I had worked myself into an eating disorder that didn't used to exist. I went from normal eating, to very restricted diet eating, to emotional eating, and then to binge eating.

What happened for me was that I literally woke up and scared myself, and I stopped binge eating just like that. I didn't know how or why I was able to stop, because this was before Katherine's approach was created. All I knew was that I was scared enough. Looking back now, I was scared enough for the part of my brain that was in charge and wanted the best for me to take over and stop responding to that urge to eat. I still struggled for a while with emotional eating, but it had nothing really to do with the binge eating, and now I don't do that anymore either. Since I found Katherine, I totally look at it in a different way.

## The basic approach

**Katherine:** The approach we'll talk about in this podcast is the Brain over Binge approach. It's a simplified approach to recovery based on some very basic and very practical brain science, and don't worry, this is nothing complicated.

It's an alternative to the mainstream approach that says binge eating is a coping mechanism or a symptom of underlying problems. The Brain over Binge approach is about learning to see your binge eating in light of the brain and then using that information to empower yourself. It's about changing your perspective to see yourself as fundamentally healthy, not broken and not diseased.

In this philosophy, binge eating is a natural but very primitive brain response to restrictive dieting, which becomes a habit over time. Binge eating can also develop as a primitive response to repeated overeating of highly stimulating foods, which increases over time and forms the same habit. So it either begins after a period of dieting as a basic survival response, which was the case for me, or it starts more like Cookie described, where overeating starts and gradually grows over time. Either way, it's a primitive brain response that makes you feel out of control.

What we're going to teach you is to take control back, using the higher parts of your brain, your higher cognitive power, to begin overcoming the binge eating habit in an efficient and uncomplicated way.

Probably the most important thing you can learn up front is that you are healthy. There isn't anything fundamentally wrong with you. You're just a temporary victim of your own healthy brain, and right now part of your brain is wired in a way that's harmful to you. This is completely correctable, but correcting it takes some understanding of how the brain works and of how you have the ability inside yourself to overcome this harmful habit.

## The one big idea

**Katherine:** The Brain over Binge approach is based around one big idea: there's only one cause of binge eating, and that is the urges to binge. I'm going to repeat that because it's very important to understand up front. The binge urge, the desire to binge, is the one and only direct cause of binge eating.

I realize this may sound overly simple, but this idea is going to streamline recovery for you. Each and every binge is preceded by an urge to binge, and no one would binge without that desire.

I define an urge to binge as any thought, any feeling, or any physical sensation that encourages binge eating. Without the urges to binge, you simply would not binge, regardless of the problems in your life, regardless of what you've been through in your past, regardless of what emotions you're feeling.

This is really the opposite of traditional therapy, which says the cause of binge eating is those emotions, those problems, those underlying issues. But really it's just this strong push from your brain to eat massive quantities of food. If you don't understand why this is happening, it's going to keep driving you to do it over and over again.

Think about a problem like depression. If you're depressed but you don't have urges to binge, you're not going to binge. The same with anxiety. These examples illustrate why emotions and problems aren't the direct cause of binge eating.

A main concept that I teach is that you binge not to cope with emotions and problems, but in order to cope with the urges to binge. The urges can make you feel so uncomfortable and so out of control that you feel like you need to binge just to make them go away.

## The two recovery goals

**Cookie:** It's so important for you to hear this. We only suggest that you focus on two things. One is dismiss the urges to binge. The second is to learn how to eat adequately.

If you dismiss the urges to binge, you will not binge. There literally will not be a problem. That's why Katherine explains it as so simple. We've all been taught in the past that it's a complicated issue, but it's the urges to binge that cause one hundred percent of the problem.

If you learn to dismiss those urges, you're acting from your higher brain. If you don't respond to those urges, they'll fade away, and then you will find that you're no longer bingeing.

Then there's eating adequately. By dieting we mean eating restrictively, where you're hungry and you're not feeding yourself enough food. If you don't stop doing that, you're highly likely to keep those urges alive and put your lower brain back in charge, because it thinks you need to binge to survive if you're hungry all the time. Extreme dieting and restriction can lead to the binge eating response, and your cravings to binge will increase. This is a normal reaction.

So the two biggest focuses are: recognize and dismiss those urges, and eat enough so that your brain doesn't think it needs to send you into survival mode by reawakening those urges.

## Common question: isn't this too simple?

**Katherine:** When hearing about the two recovery goals, a big question that often comes up is that people think it's much too simple.

You may have been told by various sources that recovery needs to be a long process, that it requires a transformation, and that it requires resolving emotional issues and complicated problems. So if you hear us say it's actually simple, you may have trouble accepting that. This is very common and we completely understand the concern, but we also want you to be open to accepting the simplicity.

It's okay if right now you still think it's a complicated process. We just want you to keep an open mind, because we think it's going to make a lot more sense as we go along. Although recovery doesn't happen the same way for everyone, it doesn't have to be a complicated, long journey. Cookie and I are both proof of that. Once you see these concepts working and making a difference in your life, you'll be more open to accepting the simplicity, and you'll be able to take control back from that lower part of your brain.

**Cookie:** We all have a certain amount of energy and focus that we're able to put on the things that are important to us. If we zero in on the most important cause-and-effect relationship in binge eating, which is the urges and following them, and then the restricting that keeps those urges alive, we get results. If you scatter, that's not to say you don't want to improve the rest of your life, or deal with emotions better, or have better relationships. Those things are all possible, but they're not necessary. If you focus on all of them at the same time, you're scattering your focus and your energy, and it's unnecessary.

So we're going to keep emphasizing the two main focuses and teach you how to keep your aim on those two things and push the other things aside while you're working on this. You can always make other changes in your life, but let's deal with the binge eating first.

**Katherine:** Yes, and then that opens up your energy and your time and allows you to make any other changes you want to make in your life.
