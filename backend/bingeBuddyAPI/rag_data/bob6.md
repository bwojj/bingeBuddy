# Episode 6 — Component 3: Stop Reacting to the Urges

*The Brain over Binge Podcast — Katherine Hansen and Cookie Rosenbloom*

---

## Welcome

**Katherine:** Hello and welcome to the Brain over Binge podcast, where we share a simple brain-based approach to ending binge eating. I'm Katherine Hansen, and I'm here again today with my friend and colleague Cookie Rosenbloom.

**Cookie:** Hi everyone, and hi Katherine. I'm really happy to be here with you today and to share what we both know and both believe in, and both have seen work, not only with all of our clients and in the groups we do together, but personally.

Many of you have heard Katherine's story, but you should also know that I have suffered with and gotten through both the beginnings of binge eating and a lot of emotional eating, and created a coaching practice around that so I could share with you what I went through and what I experienced coming out of both of those things. Today they're just a part of my past, and that's what we'd love to get through to you in these podcasts: your binge eating can be a thing of the past, and we're here to help you do exactly that.

**Katherine:** That's right, Cookie. We want to guide you toward ending binge eating for good. We know how painful it can be in your life, because as we talked about in our very first episode, we were both binge eaters ourselves. You're definitely not alone in this, but we want you to know that you can overcome it.

Today we're going to focus on the third component of dismissing urges to binge. As a review, the first component is to view the urges to binge as neurological junk. The second component is to separate the higher brain from the urges to binge. And the third component, which we'll talk about today in detail, is to stop reacting to the urges to binge.

## What "reacting" means

**Cookie:** Component three means that you're going to experience those urges as unemotionally as possible, because that's what's going to allow you to stop reacting to them.

When we say stop reacting to them, we want you to know that a reaction can be an emotional reaction, it can be a physical reaction that you actually feel in your body, or it could be a mental reaction, in what you're saying to yourself.

The reaction might be that you argue with the urge, or you debate it and get into a discussion in your head with it. Or it could be that you're becoming upset with the urge, upset that it's happening, or analyzing why is this happening to me right now, why is it coming up. Or maybe consciously trying to not think about it and trying to power through it. You might even feel your body reacting, in the sense that you might feel your muscles tensing up.

What we want you to know is that reacting, and getting upset that the urges are coming, or being really frustrated that they won't go away, makes you more uncomfortable. You're fighting something, you're resisting something. And when you're more uncomfortable, you're much more likely to want to act on those urges just to make them go away.

So the main thing we want you to realize from component three is that the goal is to make the urges comfortable enough that you can dismiss them and allow them to just pass.

## Some reactions are automatic, and that's okay

**Katherine:** Although the urges are automatic, there are some things in this process that you can control. We can control so many of our reactions, and as we've already talked about, we can also control our actions, because of our higher brain and its capacity for self-control. We'll talk about not acting on urges in more detail next week when we cover the fourth component.

But for this component, know that some reactions actually happen automatically and there isn't much we can do about it, and that's okay. You aren't going to be able to control every feeling that may come up during a binge urge. For example, you may automatically have some feelings of anxiety or frustration when the binge urge appears, and that's okay. We don't want you to feel like you have to stop every feeling that comes up. The goal is simply to not make things worse and make things a lot harder for yourself by getting really upset about this and by having a lot of other counterproductive reactions.

## Counterproductive reactions to avoid

**Katherine:** Here are some examples of counterproductive reactions you want to try to avoid.

**Engaging the urge.** You don't want to talk back to it, try to reason with it, or try to fight it. This just serves to turn attention toward the urge and make you more anxious in the process.

**Judging or criticizing yourself** for having the urge. The urge just is. It's a passing experience. It's not your fault that it's there. But the good news is you can always decide what to do when it comes up, and not acting on the urge becomes easier when you aren't judging yourself or getting really angry that the urge is there.

**Trying to make the urge go away**, or even wishing that it would go away. If you're wishing it away or trying all these things to get rid of it, you're really giving it a lot more attention and noticing it more than you otherwise would.

We want you to try to get to a place where you are okay with the urge being present and you're more accepting of it. That may seem like a big shift from things you've tried in the past and it may take some getting used to, but we believe that the less you wish the urge away and the less you try to make it go away, the quicker it's actually going to go away.

## Detachment

**Cookie:** So how do you do that without getting involved in it? The way you can make this play out is by looking at the urge with detachment.

That means that you see what's happening, you experience the urge, you're not fighting it, you're not running away from it, and you're not engaging in it. You see it happening, but it's almost as if you see it happening to someone else. You see it happening, but you don't jump in. The experience is still there, but it's almost as if you're not invested in what happens. You're just noticing it.

One of the great examples Katherine gives in her book is this: imagine that somebody is arguing with you and they have a very strong, almost violent, convincing argument, but it's about a subject that you just really don't care about. You're listening to them and maybe your mind is fading off into another subject. You're not really getting involved, you're not getting invested in what they're saying, because it doesn't really involve you.

Now, we know that you care about not bingeing anymore. But that urge is meaningless neurological junk, so we don't want you to act as if it's an argument that you need to jump in and fight and win. That's the opposite of what we want you to do. We want you to be able to stand back and just let it unfold, and then it will pass, it will die down. Just try to remain as unemotional as possible. You see it, but you're not really involved in it.

Instead of reacting and getting upset and acting on it, we want you to be able to step back and see what's happening and just think something like: I'm a person experiencing an urge. That puts one step, one space, in between you and the urge. You are a person having an urge. That's all. So you're allowing it, you're not fighting it, but nor are you invested in it and jumping in.

## Common question: how long do I have to do this?

**Katherine:** I love that explanation, Cookie. The concept of detachment is really important, and it's a concept I frequently use in my books and that we talk a lot about in our group course.

When binge eaters learn about detachment, something they often ask is: how long do I have to detach from the urge before it goes away? Or: how many times do I have to experience this urge with detachment before it starts to fade? You may be thinking, how can I detach from an urge over and over again? Maybe I can do it a few times or for a certain amount of time, but I feel like I can't keep doing it throughout the day, or over the course of many weeks or months.

This is a very understandable concern, because we know you all want to be free of binge eating, and the sooner the better. But remember, you're retraining your brain. It's likely that you've taken a very long time to develop this habit of binge eating, and now the new habit of not binge eating is going to take a while to take hold.

Thinking about when the urges may go away, or trying to figure out how long you're going to have to do this, or really wishing them away, is another form of counterproductive reaction. It's just another way you're becoming emotional about the urge and reacting to it.

When you tell yourself that you can't continue to experience the urges with detachment for any length of time, you will start to feel like you have to binge just to make the urge go away. But this is the opposite of what you want to do, because it perpetuates the habit and ensures the urges will keep coming back. When you think about how long each urge is lasting, or worry about future urges, or think about how many urges you've experienced in the past, it can make you anxious and feel like you just want to binge to get it over with. Doing so is not going to get you what you truly want, which is a life free of binge eating.

**Cookie:** This is absolutely one of the most common questions we get. How many times will I need to dismiss this in order for it to stop coming? The answer is that everybody's different. We don't have an exact number.

But the more you can stay in the here and now, the better. There are some occasions in dealing with the urges where it pays to step back and rise above and look down at the big picture and see what's happening. But in the moment of the urge, where the goal is to not attach to it and not react to it and not act on it, we want you, instead of looking at the big picture, to be right here in the moment, in the now. Not looking at how many times this has come up, how long it's going to take, how many days you've been binge free, or why did this come up now. Just be in the moment with that urge and focus on seeing what's happening, letting it flow into your awareness, but seeing it in a detached way. You're just a person experiencing an urge, and it will flow away as easily as it came up.

If you worry about when it will go away and when it will come back again, we know that's the natural thing you've been doing so far, but it's really exhausting. If you can just see what's happening, it's the opposite. It's not exhausting, it's not a fight, it doesn't take a lot of effort. So instead of saying why is this still happening, just say: I'm just a person having an urge, and it's going to pass.

**Katherine:** I just want to add that we can't guarantee when your urges will go away. Like Cookie said, it's a little different for everyone, and sometimes it depends on how long you've had this habit. But remember that if you stop worrying about when the urges will go away and stop trying to wish them away, they can actually go away faster. So as much as you can, try to accept the presence of these urges and just observe them with detachment.
