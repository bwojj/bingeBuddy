The reason we mention that right now is that we found through our one-on-one work with women and men just like you who binged, that toward the end of their recovery, once they feel like they've been able to stop acting on these urges and the urges fade away, they say: okay, but now I'm eating too much and now I'm eating emotionally, how do I deal with that? That's when we teach them the principles I write about in my book, *Clearing Your Path to Permanent Weight Loss*. It helps you uncover the reasons why you may have failed in getting rid of some of those other eating habits. That's something you may not be interested in at this moment, but you can get a download of my book for free in the show notes. In the meantime, let's focus on the binge eating habit and how we can help you totally extinguish it.

**Katherine:** In this podcast we're focusing specifically on ending the binge eating. We're sharing key ideas that we hope will empower you to overcome this habit so that you can move on with your life.

Today we're going to talk about the fourth component of dismissing urges, which is absolutely vital and the key to rewiring your brain. The fourth component is: stop acting on urges to binge.

## A note if this is your first episode

**Katherine:** I want to stop for a minute and mention that if you're listening to this podcast for the first time, you may be thinking this sounds much too simple. You may be thinking, well, if I could stop acting on my urges to binge I would be recovered by now.

If this is your first time joining us, please know that the information we'll share today about component four will make much more sense and seem much more doable in the context of everything we've discussed so far. So we encourage you to go back and listen to the previous episodes, or at least the last three, which discuss components one through three. If you've already listened, you will understand why not acting on urges is absolutely possible, and how it is possible.

## Component 4: stop taking action

**Cookie:** Component three last week was to stop reacting to the urge, and we talked about the different ways you might react mentally, emotionally and physically. Component four is to stop taking action. Stop acting on the urges to binge. Meaning, don't do it, don't follow the urge.

It's not pretending that the urge is not there. It's not acting on it. The reason this is so important is that that's what allows your brain to change. Allowing those urges to come up, seeing what they are, and not acting on them changes a pathway in your brain. That's how you literally lose the habit of bingeing. It erases the habit, literally. So this is the cure for binge eating.

We know it sounds simple, and we know it may not be simple, but the way that you can do it is by following the three components that came before this. Once you know what those urges are, once you understand that they're just neurological junk from a pathway you created by acting on them again and again and again, once you know that you're separate from them, and once you're not reacting to them anymore, then not acting on them, not following them, not going into a binge, actually becomes possible.

## What to do during an urge

**Katherine:** There's nothing specific that you need to do in order to not act on binge urges, but we want you to find what works best for you and what feels most comfortable during the times that binge urges are present, because they are going to continue to be present, since this is how your brain is conditioned right now.

For example:

- You can pick an activity to do while you're experiencing the binge urges, or choose from a list of several activities you come up with beforehand.
- You can just continue going about your day as if you're not experiencing an urge, meaning keep doing what you had planned and what you're already doing.
- You can pause and sit or lie still and observe the urge with detachment.

What's often very helpful is to keep track of your success and notice what works for you. When you focus on what works, you can build from there and gain more confidence over time.

**Cookie:** Katherine, I love that you're saying what you need to do in order to not act, because it reminds me of somebody in one of our group forums right now who talked about the act of not acting. The action we want you to take is to not act.

## Expect some discomfort

**Cookie:** One of the important things to consider is what you expect. Once you read these concepts, once you listen to us, once you've read the books, do you expect that it will just fade away and be very simple, or that the urges may not come up again?

We want you to have realistic expectations, in that it may not feel easy all the time. We're not saying it's going to be a struggle, but if you're feeling like this should just be a snap now that you have the intellectual understanding, you may be surprised if it feels uncomfortable taking these steps.

This discomfort is natural. It's a sign of going up against what you've always done, in a new way. That's what happens when we change habits. There is always discomfort as a part of change, but you need to go through the discomfort until you get to comfort. So we want you to be aware and expect some level of discomfort, and be willing to go through it.

We also want you to look at the contrast. Nobody wants discomfort, and we know you would wish it could be a different way, but the discomfort you experience when you don't act on the binge urge is temporary, and it is much less painful than the discomfort you're going to get if you act on the urge. You know what we're talking about: that post-binge discomfort that lasts much longer and is much more disruptive to your life and to how you think about yourself.

Try to remember that discomfort is normal, it will pass, and it's actually a good sign that you're heading in the right direction.

**Katherine:** That's right, and that's so important to see, that acting on the urge is a lot more uncomfortable. That's why you're listening to this podcast, that's why you're trying to recover, because it's causing so much pain in your life. When you're not acting, like Cookie said, there might be some discomfort, but if you're practicing these components and understanding them deeply, that should help relieve a lot of the discomfort, and you should be able to experience these urges with more detachment.

## What if I do binge?

**Katherine:** Remember that not acting on binge urges is a new skill that you're learning, and you may not always be perfect in using this new skill, especially when you're first starting.

When learning the fourth component, I think the most common question is: what if I do act on a binge urge, then what?

First of all, the most important thing is to avoid taking it as evidence that you're powerless and that you can't recover, because that's not the case at all. A binge only means that you acted on one urge. It doesn't mean you're destined to act on the next one or the one after that. Quite possibly you may have only binged because you didn't recognize one thought. There could have been just one tempting thought that you didn't see as neurological junk, and you followed it. When you look at it this way, it feels a lot less overwhelming and a lot less like evidence that you can't do this, because you absolutely can.

A binge doesn't mean you're back to square one. You can learn from it, you can move forward, you can recognize what happened, and you can get stronger each time.

**Cookie:** I would guess that each time you've given in to that urge to binge, you probably at some point have told yourself something like, well, now I have to start all over again. As Katherine just explained, you're never starting all over again, because you already know things that you didn't know. Once you know something, once you see something, even if you're not where you want to be yet, you can't unknow it, you can't unsee it.

So if you binge, we really urge you not to beat yourself up. It's absolutely unhelpful. Actually, when you get upset, when you have any strong emotion, that just makes that pathway in your brain a little bit stronger. So don't be upset if you've gotten upset in the past, and don't be upset with yourself for being upset.

Going forward, if you do give in to that urge and you do binge, instead just calmly, when you get to a place of calm, look back and work backwards and look at where you might have done something different. Look at the point of decision, where you decided, because it's an act of decision if you decided to go into the binge. Look at where you might have had other choices. Look to see whether maybe you were not recognizing the voice urging you to go into a binge. And make a commitment to yourself to move forward and be more aware next time. But please don't waste any time or energy beating yourself up.

This is just a new way of looking at things and a new way of not doing something, so of course you're going to fall down. You are going to make mistakes. You are going to give in sometimes. It's part of the process. It's natural.

**Katherine:** Yes, Cookie, that is so helpful. We want you to remember that everyone recovers on their own timeline. Some people are able to stop acting on the urges very quickly, and it takes some people much longer to really feel separate from the urges and capable of dismissing them. It may take a lot of, like Cookie said, falling down and getting back up and learning to do things differently, and that's perfectly okay.

So please accept where you are in this process, and just keep making progress, keep learning more about the components, keep practicing, and you will get where you want to be.
