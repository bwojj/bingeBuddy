# Episode 4 — Component 1: View the Urges as Neurological Junk

*The Brain over Binge Podcast — Katherine Hansen and Cookie Rosenbloom*

---

## Welcome

**Katherine:** This is the Brain over Binge podcast, where we share a simple brain-based approach to ending binge eating. I'm Katherine Hansen, author of *Brain over Binge* and my newer book *The Brain over Binge Recovery Guide*. I'm very glad to be co-hosting this show with master coach and author Cookie Rosenbloom.

**Cookie:** Hi everyone. I want to welcome you to our podcast and let you know that we're really happy to support you in your goal to make binge eating something that you used to do.

**Katherine:** Our goal on this podcast is to share information and insights to help enable you to quit binge eating. We want to empower you and encourage you, and we want to keep recovery as simple as possible.

If you've listened to previous episodes, you'll know that we aren't going to be focusing on your other problems. We are only going to be focusing on how to stop binge eating.

So far you've learned that the urges to binge are the only direct cause of binge eating. You've also learned that these urges come from a more primitive part of the brain, a part that's in charge of our survival drives and our habits, which we call the lower brain. You've also learned that the lower brain cannot control your actions, that you always remain in charge of what you do, because you have a higher brain. Because of the higher brain, you can learn to stop acting on the urges to binge, and then your urges can simply go away.

## The skill of dismissing urges

**Katherine:** To do this, we're going to teach you the skill of dismissing urges. Dismissing urges is the first recovery goal in the Brain over Binge approach. It means you're going to learn to have a binge urge but not give it attention and not act on it.

Until this point you may have felt like not acting on a binge urge was impossible, or at least extremely difficult. We understand that binge urges can make you feel like you have no choice, but we want to put the power of choice back in your hands. We want you to be able to choose not to act on an urge without it feeling like an impossible struggle or something unbearable or unmanageable, like it may have felt in the past.

What we're going to teach you will allow you to experience your urges in a whole new way, in a way that makes it possible to choose not to follow them. The skill of dismissing urges is about changing your perspective when the urges arise, so that you can avoid letting them drive you into automatic action.

In my books I teach five components of dismissing urges to binge, and today we're going to discuss the first component in detail.

## The five components

**Cookie:** Here they are, and we'll go into each one in detail over the next few episodes:

1. View the urges to binge as neurological junk.
2. Separate the higher brain from the urges to binge.
3. Stop reacting to urges to binge.
4. Stop acting on the urges to binge.
5. Get excited. (My favorite.)

All of these components work together, so each time you have an urge, by using them you can experience the urge in a totally different way. You're going to learn about all five one at a time, but they kind of flow together and they don't feel so distinct and separate. Once you begin to work on number one and see the urges as neurological junk, all the other steps will more easily flow after that.

We need you to know that it's not a magic formula that's going to wipe away your urges. What it is going to do is make your urges disappear if you continue to practice teaching your brain that your habit of bingeing is not necessary anymore. The way you do that is to let the urges come up and not act on them. That allows neuroplasticity to work, to change your brain and erase the habit. So you're going to have a new habit of seeing the urges and not acting. It will come up, you will see it, you will not act on it, and then it will start to come up less often and with less intensity, until it's just pruned away from lack of use.

## Component 1: neurological junk

**Katherine:** The first component of dismissing urges is to view the urges as neurological junk. This means you're not going to be giving the urges value or attention.

Neurological junk means kind of just what it sounds like. It's junk from the lower brain. It's just some neurons firing in a way that they shouldn't be, and in a way that's not meaningful to you.

The binge urges don't signal a real need or something that you truly want. They're an automatic signal from the lower brain, because right now your primitive brain thinks you need to binge to survive. It senses that you need to maintain this habit, but you actually know better.

So as soon as you hear the thoughts encouraging you to binge, as soon as you feel this feeling and this craving, we want you to immediately label it as junk from the lower brain.

This is very different from a lot of the traditional recommendations you may have heard. In other approaches, common advice is to really analyze the urges and discover their deeper meaning. For example, do the urges mean you have unresolved stress and you're feeling a need to binge to cope? Does it mean you're feeling lonely and you want a binge to comfort yourself? We don't feel viewing the urges this way is helpful in most cases. Instead of analyzing the urge, we want you to see it as just a faulty brain signal that means nothing important to you. There's no deeper meaning. There's nothing in the urge that means something to your higher self.

Your goal with this step is to view that urge as meaningless, as harmless and as powerless. We know this is different from the way you probably experience it right now. We want you to try to view the urge as non-threatening and experience it without a lot of fear and without a lot of anxiety. What we don't want you to do is fight it, and we don't want you to try to run away from it.

## What not to do

**Cookie:** With this first component you're not necessarily doing anything specific. What we want you to do is try to see the urge differently. There are specific things we're going to ask you not to do. You're not going to be giving the urges consideration. You're not going to make them important. Give them no value, give them no attention. It's more a matter of seeing them differently than doing something differently.

No engaging, no negotiation. We don't want you to try to decipher the meaning of the urges or try to analyze them. No debates.

Think about it this way. When you get an urge and you respond to it, it's like somebody is hitting you a tennis ball on a tennis court and you hit the ball back automatically. That's what you do when you respond to the urge. We want you to think that you're not going to get on that tennis court and hit that ball back. The urge will come up, but you're not going to respond to it.

**Katherine:** We want you to get to a place where, as soon as you feel that first sensation, that first feeling, that first thought that encourages binge eating, you immediately and deeply see it as junk from the lower brain. What that can do is really prevent the urge from building and strengthening.

Even if you did give the urges attention and value in the past, and thought they signaled some deeper meaning like an emotional need, remember that they're no longer serving you. These urges and this binge eating habit are causing pain in your life. And these urges really aren't coming from you at all. They're coming from a primitive part of your brain that is separate from your higher self.

So component one is all about seeing the urge differently, in a way that makes it feel less compelling. If you can learn to view these urges as neurological junk, you'll already feel much less tempted to act on them.

## Common issue: giving some thoughts value

**Katherine:** In our group course we see a lot of the common issues that come up. When learning about the first component, what tends to happen is that some people view some urge thoughts and feelings as junk, but then give value and attention to other thoughts and feelings.

For example, you may be doing well dismissing urges, but then you may have a thought like: just binge one last time and then you can start over. Or: you need to binge to relax after this hard day. Or: you've already eaten some unhealthy food today, so you might as well binge. Suddenly you may find yourself starting to believe those thoughts, and you might even follow them and binge.

What we want you to do is not give any of these thoughts value. We don't want you to give anything that promotes binge eating any attention, regardless of the actual content of the thought. The content of the thought doesn't matter. It's the fact that it's encouraging you to binge. The goal is to view any thought, any feeling, any physical sensation that encourages binge eating as neurological junk.

**Cookie:** It's interesting, Katherine, people will believe that this one is different, that this urge feels real, this one feels special.

Sometimes the thoughts you give value to and don't recognize as neurological junk are the thoughts that say you need to binge to cope, or to relax, or to have some stress relief. In the moment we know these thoughts can be tempting, but inevitably after the binge you always realize that it didn't give you any of those benefits and it only made things worse.

Always try to remember that the only real relief you will ever get from bingeing is relief from the urge. But bingeing to get relief from the urge only reinforces it and ensures that it will keep coming back, and your habit will get stronger and stronger.

So if you can apply this first component, urges are going to begin to feel more manageable, so that you can accept the presence of the urge and allow it to pass, and see it for what it is, without feeling like you have to act on it just to make it go away.

When that urge comes up, we know it seems like it's offering to help you. If you do feel stressed or overwhelmed, you may think you need relief, and it's offering you relief. It's so tempting. But know that that relief is false, because it's being offered by your lower brain, and always remember that every time you act on it you're just reinforcing it.

**Katherine:** Exactly. It just makes everything worse. Any problems you are having, it makes them even more unmanageable. So if you can learn to view the urges as junk, it can really transform the way you relate to them and give you the ability to stop acting on them.
