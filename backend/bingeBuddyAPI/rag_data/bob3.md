# Episode 3 — The Lower Brain and the Higher Brain

*The Brain over Binge Podcast — Katherine Hansen and Cookie Rosenbloom*

---

## Welcome

**Katherine:** Welcome to the Brain over Binge podcast, where we share a simple brain-based approach to ending binge eating. This is Katherine Hansen, author of *Brain over Binge* and *The Brain over Binge Recovery Guide*, and I'm so glad to be co-hosting this show with my friend and colleague Cookie Rosenbloom.

**Cookie:** Thank you Katherine, I'm excited to be here.

**Katherine:** Cookie is a master coach and an author herself, and we also teach a course together using this approach.

Today we're going to talk about the lower and the higher brain. This is a central part of the Brain over Binge approach: that we actually have two parts of our brain at work in binge eating.

## Where the urges come from

**Katherine:** Last episode we talked about the urges to binge and how they are the direct cause of binge eating. In this episode we want to tell you that these urges come from a lower, more primitive part of your brain, which some people call the animal brain. This part of your brain is in charge of your survival instincts and your habits.

As we talked about last show, these urges arise because of survival instincts, which a lot of times are caused by dieting and calorie restriction, or they arise because of a habit you've conditioned over time.

These processes, survival and habit, come from a lower part of your brain. It comes from deep inside your brain, an older part of your brain if we're talking evolutionary history. This part of our brain is similar to the brain of animals, and that's why some people call it the animal brain. In my books I call it the lower brain. Just knowing that these urges develop in this lower, more instinctual part of your brain can really help you feel like you have power over them.

**Cookie:** We know that you may not be a big fan of brain science, and that's okay. But it's really important to know that the urges come from that lower part of your brain, because if you know that, you know that it's not you, the real you, who wants the best for yourself and wants to be happy and live a good life.

It's empowering, because you'll understand that those urges are the product of a primitive system, the part of your brain that's been around for a very long time, and those urges don't really signal what you truly want or need. Of course you want more from your life than the pain that binge eating brings you. When the urge comes up you may think you have no choice, and you may feel helpless, because you believe the urges mean something. So if you understand where those urges come from and why you get them, that's what's going to allow you to not act on them when they come up. It's empowering.

## The higher brain

**Katherine:** That's right, Cookie. The most important thing is to know that you have the ability to not act on binge urges, because where your true self lies is in your higher brain.

The higher brain is what we associate with our identity and with our goals. It's basically our more rational self, our more human self. More specifically, that rational human self lies in a part of the higher brain called the prefrontal cortex. This is a newer part of the brain if we're talking evolutionary history, and it's the part that gives you the ability to have self-control, to veto impulses that come from the lower brain. The lower brain sends a lot of instinctual and habitual responses, but we always have a choice in our higher brain to say no to those impulses.

The binge urge is an impulse. It's a primitive, animalistic impulse signaling that you need to binge to survive, because that's the way your lower brain is conditioned right now. But you, in your higher brain, know better. You know that binge eating is causing pain in your life. You know that you want better for yourself. You know that you want to recover.

The reason you have this ability to overcome binge urges is based on some more very simple brain science. The higher brain gives humans the ability to choose what to focus on and what actions to take based on the information coming from the lower brain. The higher brain contains our conscious awareness and, most importantly for the purposes of dismissing urges, it contains our self-control functions.

Your higher brain is ultimately in control of when and how you use your voluntary muscles. This is very important, because the lower brain cannot move voluntary muscles by itself. It needs the higher brain to act, or to go along with it. If you have an impulse, your higher brain basically has to say yes in order for your voluntary muscles to move.

So all the lower brain can do is send out urges. It's actually powerless to make you act. You can only decide to act with your higher brain.

A lot of times in binge eating you may feel out of control. You may feel like the urge leaves you no choice, or that the urge is driving you to do something you just can't seem to control. But it's not a true loss of control. It's a perceived feeling of losing control. You still retain the capacity to choose, and understanding how and why you have this ability makes it possible to start putting your higher brain back in the driver's seat and start making choices that are in your best interest.

If you can become more aware of where these urges come from, and that you don't have to act on them because you ultimately have control of your voluntary muscles, it empowers you to take control back. You can start allowing the urge to be present without acting on it, and by not acting you start to rewire your brain and change this habit.

## You have to want to recover

**Cookie:** You really have to want to recover for this method to work. And when we say you, we mean you, the real you, your higher brain. The higher version of you has to want to recover.

If you've ever listened to Katherine before in various interviews, or read her books, or taken one of our courses, you should know that we have never met anyone who is bingeing who does not want to recover. We know that you do. The reason that's so important is that your higher brain has to get the message that this is not what you want in your life.

Focusing on why you want to recover can be really helpful to keep you on track. So we suggest that you begin to think about, or even write down, the ways your life would be better, not perfect but better, if you no longer binged.

If you know you want to recover, and we know that you do, you'll be better able to recognize as the urges form that they're coming from your lower brain. You'll be able to see the urges and know that they hold no truth. They're going to become less difficult to see and not act on. The important thing is that you focus on your desire to be totally done with binge eating.

## False wanting

**Katherine:** Yes, your higher brain has to be on board with recovery for this method to be effective. But what we want you to understand is that this doesn't mean you have to feel like you want to recover at every second, because during an urge to binge it's the lower brain's job to make you feel like you have to binge and that you need to binge. So during those times you are going to feel a sense of wanting the binge, and you might not feel as well connected with your desire for recovery.

This is what I call false wanting. This is not what you truly want. This is what, in the moments of the urge, the lower brain tries to convince you that you want, simply because that's its job. If you can allow this feeling of wanting to pass, knowing that it's only what your lower brain has been conditioned to make you feel, you will again reconnect with your higher brain's desire to recover.

An important thing to know is that the lower brain is only concerned with temporary pleasure and with maintaining this habit it thinks you need to survive. The lower brain is not concerned with your goals or your plans or what you want for yourself now or in the future. That's your higher brain's job. It's not that your lower brain has any malice against you. It's simply responsible for immediate reward and immediate pleasure, for meeting survival needs in the moment, and for maintaining habits. Those are vital brain functions that we do need, and when they're working properly they serve us well. The problem simply comes in when the lower brain becomes conditioned to react as if a harmful substance or harmful behavior is necessary for survival.

The urge to binge in the moment will tell you that you do want this and that you don't want to recover, but this is just a glitch in your lower brain that you can learn to rise above.

## If you have trouble recognizing the lower brain

**Katherine:** When Cookie and I teach these concepts, some people are able to recognize the difference in their two brains right away and it just clicks. Others don't recognize the lower brain quite as quickly, and they may continue believing that the lower brain is expressing their true wants and needs, and keep following it.

In this situation I recommend taking a good look at what is leading up to the binge. I like people to write down what thoughts they're experiencing before a binge, what physical sensations they're experiencing, and what feelings are making them feel like they want to binge, like those feelings of desire and false wanting.

Writing all this down so you can see what's actually encouraging you to binge helps you become a lot more aware of what's going on. Otherwise it can seem like the lower brain just takes over and you don't have much of a choice.

If you start becoming aware and looking at this process, you'll see that a binge doesn't just come out of nowhere. There's always a feeling of wanting, or a tempting thought, or a physical craving, and likely all three, prior to you starting to binge.

When you write these things down, you will see that all of these thoughts and feelings encouraging binge eating actually don't make any sense. Your higher brain doesn't actually believe these thoughts at all. You only temporarily believe them when you're caught up in an urge. If you look at what you wrote down when you're not having an urge, you can clearly see with your higher brain that those thoughts really aren't you. When you do this, it's the beginning of seeing that you are not your lower brain, and that you don't actually want what it's been conditioned to drive you toward.

**Cookie:** Recognizing that lower brain when it starts to go into action is so crucial. One of the ways of increasing your ability to recognize what's happening, so you don't just automatically go into what you've usually done, is really focusing on your desire to recover. The more you can feel that you truly don't want this in your life, the easier it will be to recognize that lower brain when it gives you the urges.

## Looking honestly at the cost

**Cookie:** Part of this is the ability to recognize the consequences of what happens when you do follow the urge. If you do binge, we do not want you to beat yourself up for it at all. But all the negative feelings you create afterward are helpful, because they're going to help you see why you want to recover. All the regret you feel after a binge, the shame. We don't want to encourage you to feel bad about yourself at all, but those bad feelings are a good reminder of why you do not want to keep doing this.

So we want you to take an honest look at the cost of doing the behavior, so you can see really clearly that it's not worth it to you. Your lower brain may tell you that the binge will feel good or taste good or be worth it, and that is a tiny fraction of temporary pleasure. And that tiny temporary pleasure is actually ruining what could be an incredible life for you.

We want you to compare that little temporary pleasure to all the positives you would get if you stopped binge eating, and focus on that. The temporary pleasure is not what you really want. When you focus on stopping and on what you will get from that, it can open your life up to all kinds of possibilities.

Katherine and I have both seen the amazing things that happen to people when they are able to see their urges, understand that it's not really what they want, just a habit, and not give in to them. It is truly amazing.

## Wrap-up

**Katherine:** What we hope is that this episode will help you get in touch with your higher brain and connect with your desire for recovery, and then disconnect from the habit that's in the lower brain and from these urges that are giving you this false wanting and this false feeling of need.

I also want to note that you're not disconnecting from your lower brain altogether. You will still have pleasure in your life and you'll still have pleasure in eating, but it's not going to be a pleasure that harms you. Binge eating is not true pleasure. If that pleasure was worth it to you, you wouldn't be listening to this podcast and you wouldn't be trying to recover. Binge eating is ruining your plans and your goals and all the things that your higher brain wants.

So we want you to see more and more that the urges are not speaking truth for you. They're not telling you what you truly want or truly need. We know for sure that you can do this, and we're here to help you do it.

That wraps up today's episode about the lower and the higher brain. Next time we'll teach you more about the first goal of recovery, which is dismissing urges to binge. Remember, in the Brain over Binge approach there are two recovery goals: dismissing urges and eating adequately. When we talk about dismissing urges there are five parts, or what I call five components. In the next episode we'll be talking about the first component and how to apply it.

**Katherine and Cookie:** You have the power to rewire your brain and live a binge-free life.
